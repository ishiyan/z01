# Hedging Calibration Scale

Match language strength to evidence strength. Overclaiming (strong words, weak evidence) erodes trust. Underclaiming (weak words, strong evidence) buries impact.

## Five Levels

| Level | Evidence Type | Appropriate Language |
|-------|--------------|---------------------|
| 1 — Definitive | Direct, replicated, mechanistic proof | "demonstrates", "establishes", "proves", "is" |
| 2 — Strong | Strong indirect, consistent correlational, multiple lines | "shows", "indicates", "reveals", "confirms" |
| 3 — Moderate | Single study, moderate effect, reasonable inference | "suggests", "supports", "is consistent with", "points to" |
| 4 — Preliminary | Limited data, early-stage, small sample | "may", "might", "could", "appears to", "seems to" |
| 5 — Speculative | Beyond available data, theoretical, extrapolation | "conceivably", "potentially", "we speculate", "one possibility is" |

## Diagnosis

| Problem | Pattern | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Overclaiming | Level 4 evidence + Level 1 language | Weaken the verb |
| Underclaiming | Level 1 evidence + Level 4 language | Strengthen the verb |
| Hedge stacking | Multiple hedges in one sentence | Pick one appropriate hedge |
| False confidence | No evidence stated + definitive language | Add evidence or downgrade |

## Examples

- **Overclaim**: "This proves that X causes Y" (based on one correlational study)
  - Fix: "This suggests that X may contribute to Y"
- **Underclaim**: "It might possibly be the case that water boils at 100C"
  - Fix: "Water boils at 100C at standard pressure"
- **Hedge stack**: "It could potentially perhaps indicate..."
  - Fix: "This may indicate..."
