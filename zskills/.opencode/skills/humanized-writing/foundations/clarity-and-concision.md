# Clarity and Concision

Adapted from William Strunk Jr.'s *The Elements of Style* (1918) — the canonical authority on English prose.

## Elementary Rules of Usage

1. **Possessives:** Form the possessive singular by adding `'s` (Charles's friend, Burns's poems). Exceptions: ancient proper names ending in -es/-is (Jesus', Isis').
2. **Series comma:** In a series of three or more, use a comma after each term except the last (red, white, and blue).
3. **Parenthetic expressions:** Enclose between commas. If the interruption is slight, omit commas.
4. **Coordinating conjunction:** Place comma before `and`, `but`, `or`, `nor`, `for`, `so`, `yet` when introducing an independent clause.
5. **No comma splice:** Do not join independent clauses with a comma alone. Use a semicolon, conjunction, or separate sentences.
6. **No sentence fragments (as errors):** Do not break sentences in two where the second part cannot stand alone — unless used deliberately for emphasis.
7. **Dangling modifiers:** A participial phrase at the beginning must refer to the grammatical subject. "Walking to the store, the rain started" is wrong.

## Elementary Principles of Composition

### 8. One paragraph, one topic
The beginning of each paragraph signals a new step. Examine single-topic paragraphs to see if subdivision improves them.

### 9. Begin with topic sentence; end with emphasis
The topic sentence lets readers know what the paragraph is about. The final position carries weight.

### 10. Use active voice
Active voice is direct, vigorous, and concise.
- Passive: "The meeting was led by the director."
- Active: "The director led the meeting."

Use passive only when the actor is unknown or irrelevant.

### 11. Put statements in positive form
Say what things are, not what they aren't.
- Weak: "He was not very often on time." 
- Strong: "He usually came late."
- Weak: "did not think it was honest" 
- Strong: "thought it dishonest"

### 12. Use definite, specific, concrete language
Prefer the specific to the general, the definite to the vague, the concrete to the abstract.
- Vague: "A period of unfavorable weather set in."
- Concrete: "It rained every day for a week."

### 13. Omit needless words
Vigorous writing is concise. Every word must tell.

| Wordy | Concise |
|-------|---------|
| the question as to whether | whether |
| there is no doubt but that | no doubt / doubtless |
| used for fuel purposes | used for fuel |
| he is a man who | he |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject which | this subject |
| the reason why is that | because |
| owing to the fact that | since / because |
| in spite of the fact that | although |
| call your attention to the fact that | remind you / notify you |
| the fact that he had not succeeded | his failure |
| the fact that I had arrived | my arrival |

### 14. Avoid loose sentences
Do not string together clause after clause with `and`, `but`, `so`. Tighten.

### 15. Express coordinate ideas in similar form
Parallel construction reinforces parallel meaning.
- Wrong: "The French, the Italians, Spanish, and Portuguese"
- Right: "The French, the Italians, the Spanish, and the Portuguese"

### 16. Keep related words together
The subject and principal verb should not be separated by a phrase that can be moved.
- Awkward: "Toni Morrison, in her novel *Beloved*, portrays..."
- Better: "In her novel *Beloved*, Toni Morrison portrays..."

### 17. Keep to one tense in summaries
Use present tense for summaries of literature or ongoing truths.

### 18. Place emphatic words at the end
The end of a sentence is its strongest position.
- Weak: "Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways."
- Strong: "Since that time, humanity has advanced in many ways, but it has hardly advanced in fortitude."
